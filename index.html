The Lungs of Our Planet

The Amazon rainforest spans across nine South American countries, with the majority in Brazil. Covering over 5.5 million square kilometers, it represents over half of the planet's remaining rainforests and is the most biodiverse tract of tropical rainforest in the world.

Amazon Rainforest Location

The Amazon basin spans across Brazil, Peru, Colombia, Venezuela, Ecuador, Bolivia, Guyana, Suriname, and French Guiana

Flora of the Amazon

The Amazon rainforest contains an astonishing diversity of plant life, with over 40,000 plant species, including 16,000 tree species. These plants form complex ecosystems and provide essential resources for the forest's inhabitants.

Giant Water Lily

Victoria amazonica has leaves that can grow over 3 meters in diameter and support the weight of a small child.

Rubber Tree

Hevea brasiliensis is the primary source of natural rubber and can grow up to 40 meters tall.

Brazil Nut Tree

Bertholletia excelsa produces large, woody fruits containing nutritious seeds that are an important food source.

Fauna of the Amazon

The Amazon is home to an incredible variety of animal life, including more than 2.5 million insect species, 2,000 bird species, and over 400 mammal species. Many of these species are found nowhere else on Earth.

Jaguar

The largest cat in the Americas and a keystone species in the Amazon ecosystem.

Scarlet Macaw

Mighty Rivers

The Amazon River system is the world's largest drainage system, with over 1,100 tributaries, 17 of which are longer than 1,600 kilometers. This vast network of rivers shapes the landscape and provides essential resources for the forest and its inhabitants.

Amazon River

The largest river by discharge volume in the world, stretching approximately 6,400 km.

Rio Negro

The largest left tributary of the Amazon and the world's largest blackwater river.

Madeira River

Indigenous Tribes

The Amazon rainforest is home to more than 400 distinct indigenous groups, each with their own language, culture, and territory. These tribes have lived sustainably in the rainforest for thousands of years, possessing invaluable knowledge about the forest ecosystem.

Yanomami

One of the largest relatively isolated tribes in South America, living in northern Brazil and southern Venezuela.

Awá

Known as the world's most threatened tribe, they live as nomadic hunter-gatherers in Brazil.

Tikuna

The largest indigenous group in the Brazilian Amazon, known for their intricate art and mask-making traditions.
